The Formation and Growth of Galaxies

I study the formation and evolution of galaxies throughout the history of the universe, from the first generation of galaxies to form after the Big Bang to the diverse and complex galaxy population in the present-day universe. I primarily use spectroscopy to characterize the physical properties of gas and stars in galaxies, and use that information to understand the growth of galaxies and the evolution of the interstellar medium.

Metallicity and Baryon Cycling

The metallicity of the interstellar medium (ISM) is a powerful probe of the formation histories and growth processes of galaxies. Metallicity is the abundance of heavy elements (i.e., oxygen) relative to hydrogen. The theoretical picture for galaxy growth involves a cycle of gas into and out of galaxies known as the cycle of baryons. In this framework, unenriched gas is accreted from the intergalactic medium, building up a gas reservoir in the galaxy. This gas reservoir forms stars as it cools, and can also fall into the center of a galaxy and feed a supermassive black hole. Star formation produces heavy elements through nucleosynthesis and injects metals into the ISM as stars die in supernovae. Supernovae, stellar winds, and accreting black holes injects energy into ISM gas. This energetic feedback heats up gas, regulates the star formation rate (SFR), and drives outflows that eject gas and metals from the galaxy.

Key baryon cycling parameters including the gas outflow and inflow rates can be constrained using scaling relations between ISM metallicity and galaxy properties such as stellar mass and star-formation rate. My work has characterized the mass-metallicity relation and demonstrated the existence of a mass-metallicity-SFR relation (i.e., Fundamental Metallicity Relation; FMR) at high redshifts. The form of these metallicity scaling relations as a function of redshift informs us about the processes governing galaxy growth at different cosmic epochs.

Evolving Ionized ISM Conditions

Emission lines encode a wealth of information about the physical properties of ionized gas and the massive stars that produce the ionizing photons. Using emission-line spectra, I characterize the evolution of ISM properties out to high redshifts. I have shown that, at high redshift, the ionized ISM is denser, massive stars produce harder ionizing spectra, and chemical abundance patterns are different compared to what is seen in typical local galaxies. Understanding how changes in the ISM conditions relate to evolving galaxy properties such as SFR, age, and metallicity can reveal the physical mechanisms that drive ISM evolution.

My papers about evolving ISM properties and ionization conditions:
Sanders et al. 2016a | Sanders et al. 2020a | Sanders et al. 2020b

Metallicity Calibrations

It is important that our methods for deriving chemical abundances from observations are accurate, otherwise the results about galaxy growth will be biased. As a consequence of the evolving ISM conditions described above, we need to use different calibrations at low and high redshifts to determine galaxy metallicities. I am using deep spectroscopy to detect faint "auroral" emission lines for z~2 galaxies, with which I derive temperature-based "direct" metallicities that can be used to construct new calibrations that are appropriate for high-redshift galaxies.

My papers about metallicity calibrations at high redshift:
Sanders et al. 2016b | Sanders et al. 2020a | Sanders et al. 2020c

Diffuse Ionized Gas

Emission lines in galaxy spectra originate not only from star-forming HII regions, but can also come from other phases of the ionized ISM. One of these phases is the diffuse ionized gas (DIG), a warm and partially-ionized ISM phase extending throughout the disks of star-forming galaxies. DIG emission contributes strongly to emission lines in normal z=0 star-forming galaxies, but is not thought to be important at high redshifts. Thus, we must understand how to identify and remove DIG emission from local galaxy spectra to make robust comparisons between high and low redshift samples. I have demonstrated that the emission-line ratios of local galaxies can be described as a mixture of HII and DIG regions, with DIG responsible for roughly half of the total line emission.

Sanders et al. 2017

Collaborations

The MOSDEF survey

The MOSFIRE Deep Evolution Field (MOSDEF) survey obtained rest-frame optical spectra of 1,500 galaxies at z=1.4-3.8 using the MOSFIRE spectrograph on the 10-meter Keck I telescope. I have been a member of the MOSDEF survey team since 2014.

Liger

I am the extragalactic science lead on the Liger instrument. Liger is a planned next-generation near-infrared integral field spectrograph for Keck Observatory. It is currently in the design phase.

KAPA

I am a member of the galaxy evolution science team for the Keck All-sky Precision Adaptive Optics (KAPA) project. KAPA is a major upgrade to the Keck adaptive optics systems, and is currently underway.
